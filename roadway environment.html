    The roadway environment

    Definitions

    Road

    The entire right-of-way comprising a common or public thoroughfare, including a highway, street, bridge and any other structure incidental thereto.

    Roadway

    Quote

    That part of the road that is improved, designed or ordinarily used for the passage of vehicular traffic, inclusive of the shoulder.

    Parking on the roadway

    Parking must be done first in a legal parking spot and parking must not be done on the roadway if there is a legal spot available and you have less than 125 meters visibility each way.

    Quote

    170 (1) No person shall park, stand or stop a vehicle on a roadway,

    (a) when it is practicable to park, stand or stop the vehicle off the roadway; or

    (b) when it is not practicable to park, stand or stop the vehicle off the roadway unless a clear view of the vehicle and of the roadway for at least 125 metres beyond the vehicle may be obtained from a distance of at least 125 metres from the vehicle in each direction upon the highway. R.S.O. 1990, c. H.8, s. 170 (1).

    Performing temporary work on the roadway

    When performing temporary work we are responsible for affecting the flow of traffic and we have to consider in the traffic control plan how much we are affecting the traffic and for how long. This will along with other factors affect which layouts we choose to use.

    Work encroachment

    There are degrees of encroachment needed when working on the roadway that are determined by how much room the work needs to occupy in the roadway.

    Shoulder work

    Shoulder work refers to temporary work that takes place on the shoulder of a highway. This work does not encroach on the roadway but still needs to use traffic control devices to make the public aware of your presence.

    Lane encroachment

    When temporary work is required to intrude on the active lane but still maintains the lanes width as 3 meters or greater the temporary work is encroaching on the lane.

    Lane closure

    When temporary work reduces the active lane to less than 3 meters the traffic will need to be alternated to ensure safe passage of public vehicles as it is now unsafe to allow cars to pass each other as normal.

    Duration of work

    This is the time that the work is expected to take and include the following.

    Note

    All durations include the time to setup and takedown equipment and materials.

    Number Description
    1 Mobile Operations
    2 Intermittent Duration (ID)
    3 Very Short Duration (VSD)
    4 Short Duration (SD)
    5 Long Duration (LD)

    Mobile operations

    Mobile operations involve work that is done while continuously moving, usually at low speeds (typically 5 to 30 km/h).

    • During mobile operations, the advanced warning area moves with the activity area.
    • Mobile operations may have periodic brief stops related to the mobile activity which do not exceed a few minutes in duration.
    • During a brief stop, no planned work takes place outside of the work vehicle.
    • The devices required for mobile operations depend on the volume and speed of traffic.
    • In areas with light volume and good visibility, a well-marked, well-signed vehicle may be enough.
    • In areas with higher volume and/or speeds, a BV equipped with a flashing arrow board should follow the work vehicle.

    Intermittent duration work

    ID work occupies a fixed location for 15 minutes or less.

    • The work site may be moved along the highway and make frequent and short stops where planned work takes place outside of the work vehicle.
    • If a short stop of less than 15 minutes is required at a specific location, it is ID work rather than a Mobile Operation.
    • If a worker is to be exposed to traffic for more than 15 minutes, including the time required to set up traffic control devices and work time, then a greater protection is required, and traffic control layouts for VSD work should be used.
    • Active devices, such as flashing arrow boards and simplified setup and removal procedures, are recommended for ID work, which help to ensure adequate traffic control and reduction in the time that the worker is exposed to traffic hazards.

    Very short duration work

    VSD work occupies a fixed location for 30 minutes or less, but more than 15 minutes.

    • The work site may be moved along the highway and make frequent and short stops where planned work takes place outside of the work vehicle.
    • If a short stop is required at a specific location, it is VSD work rather than a Mobile Operation.
    • If a worker is to be exposed to traffic for more than 30 minutes, including the time required to set up traffic control devices and work time, then a greater protection is required, and traffic control layouts for SD work should be used.
    • Active devices, such as flashing arrow boards, simplified setup and removal procedures, and rolling closures, are recommended for VSD work, which help to ensure adequate traffic control, reduction in the time that the worker is exposed to traffic hazards, and more efficient and productive work operations.
    • Examples of VSD work include some utility works, minor highway maintenance, pothole patching, surveying, and stormwater catch basin cleanout.

    Short duration work

    SD work refers to activities that require work areas that are continuously occupied by workers and/or equipment for more than 30 minutes but less than one 24-hour period in duration.

    • SD work does not generally include work at a site that extends beyond 24 hours; such work is LD.
    • However, work at the same location may extend over more than one day and still be considered SD work for the purpose of traffic control device layout if all of the following conditions are met:
    • The approval of the road authority is obtained. The road authority may consider that LD work is more appropriate for recurring lane closures.
    • Any additional conditions stipulated by the road authority, including working hours, are complied with.
    • Traffic control layouts for SD work should be used if a worker is to be exposed to traffic for more than 30 minutes, including the time required to set up traffic control devices and work time.
    • Temporary highways and barriers may be provided, and inappropriate markings that cause driver confusion should be removed and replaced with temporary markings.
    • Examples of SD work include pavement marking, sign installation, and guardrail repair.

    Long duration work

    LD work refers to activities that require a work area for longer than 24 hours.

    • Longer exposure of workers and highway users requires more positive guidance through a temporary condition.
    • Temporary highways and barriers may be provided, and inappropriate markings that cause driver confusion should be removed and replaced with temporary markings.
    • For stationary operations on freeways longer than five days in duration, temporary construction barrier systems (TCBS) are required, as required by Ontario Regulation 213/91, Section 67 under the OSHA.
    • For minimal working widths, TCBS either do not deflect or have minimal deflection.
    • TCBS are designed and crash-tested for placement on paved surfaces.
    • The highway (and sidewalk) should be restored to the satisfaction of the road authority and returned to normal operation when the daily work shift (or authorized working period) is complete.
